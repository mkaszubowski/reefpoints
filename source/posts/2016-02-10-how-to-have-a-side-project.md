Pretty much every designer I know has a side project (or five). They are often in a field related to design, but do not replicate exactly what they do at their full time job. Side projects and hobbies are great for relaxing, while adding extra skills or addressing a passion that does not yet have a place in one's career.

Here‚Äôs what I learned about what works well, and what can be problematic for side projects I‚Äôve had over the years. Hope it helps you in your own creative pursuit!

## 1: Different enough
To let your brain rest and relax, a side project should be different enough from what you do during the day. When a day‚Äôs work is mostly at the computer, I feel a need to switch to analog afterwards, and still have lots of energy. If this is a [design sprint](https://dockyard.com/design-sprints) week, and I‚Äôm on my feet most of the day, sketching or writing on the whiteboard - then curling up on my couch with a laptop feels like a welcome break. Essentially, I have different types of energy I could use up during the day, and switching between activities allows me to get more done.

Another principle to keep in mind is to avoid conflicting interests between work and personal projects. When they are different enough, there is a clear separation between the two. I won‚Äôt resent filling my week-ends with the personal project, because it does not feel like work. It feels like play!

Sports or dance make a great hobby & side project. I used to spend hours each week rock climbing, then switched to social dancing a few years later. It may seem like there‚Äôs no overlap in the skills - but the ability to clearly lead and follow (typical Tango dance roles) within a larger group setting is a great communication skill for a team of designers as well. It also helped me learn my limitations. When you‚Äôre several rope-lengths up a cliff and need to tell something to other climbers on the team without seeing them, my voice never seemed loud enough to get across. Since then, I always think of a backup communication method. Do two tugs on the rope mean ‚Äúgo‚Äù? You only know if you‚Äôve arranged it ahead of time.


## 2: Similar enough

When work and play are in related disciplines, you get a lot of benefit in combining the two. For example, my photography hobby provided a ton of material for my design work - material that I owned and could art-direct myself. Hours of practicing lighting and portrait photography on the weekend allowed me to quickly organize consistent and flattering staff photos at work. Photography wasn‚Äôt exactly in my job description, but it gave me better material to design the website.

An active pursuit of two related disciplines can **act as a multiplier**, where they feed off each other. Hobbies can ‚Äúfeed‚Äù on my primary work as well: I usually take time to design a logo and a few other pieces for each side project.


## 3: Frustrated? Pick just one

One problem with side projects is that they often compete for time with chores, rest and relaxation. And if I have too many side projects at the same time, the apparent lack of progress can be frustrating. At some point I remember I was doing a half-day photo shoot every other weekend (if you do photography, you‚Äôll know how much extra time it takes to sort and process the raw photos), had a pretty involved sewing project going (which takes both time and physical space), was learning French twice a week, and attempted to do a daily watercolor illustration. Oh, and a full time job with a lengthy commute.

![sewing project](https://i.imgur.com/aReedp3.jpg)
*I miss sewing projects, but stopped doing them for the time being so I can focus on one thing at a time, with higher quality and time investment*.

I would write an overly optimistic to-do list for my week-end, but only get about halfway through it. I was probably less effective at my day job than I could have been, due to lack of sleep. My side projects felt stagnant because there wasn‚Äôt enough happening in each one from week to week. With these projects, the end goal was to produce a portfolio of creative work to apply to grad school. But I felt trapped because even with hard work, I wasn‚Äôt making enough progress.

I eventually climbed out of this. I negotiated Fridays off at work, trained the designer who would replace me, put together a portfolio and moved to Canada for a year to study. The experience left me with a better understanding of how important focus really is.


## Focus

I now know I can‚Äôt get good results from trying to juggle several side projects at a time. But deciding to do just one thing can feel scary. I often wonder if I'll ever get to work on my other projects. But it‚Äôs the only sure way to make progress.

![Color blocks and sticky notes](https://i.imgur.com/kIUHUpm.jpg)
*Johannes Itten‚Äôs classic Elements of Color (which in forms both art and design) alongside some sticky notes from my UX design life*.

In 2015, I decided to focus on one side project: my exploration of the [overlap between design and modern art](http://www.designartpractice.com/). I put away my sewing machines, and set my Etsy shop in holiday mode. I even stopped nagging myself to keep practicing with watercolors or hand lettering. I now have a more realistic to-do list, and can feel ‚Äúdone‚Äù and accomplished with the weekly illustrated articles that I plan to write for a year.
I‚Äôll be honest: I miss the other side projects I had put on hold. But am OK with not doing them for a while so that once I get to them, I‚Äôll be able to make progress and commit to giving those other passions the focused time they deserve.
